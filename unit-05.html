Conditional Derivations

Basics

  • A conditional derivation is a formal representation of a conditional proof.
  • Conditional proof is a familiar pattern of hypothetical reasoning.
  • The basic idea: to prove an if…then…, assume that the if… is true, and show that, from this assumption, the then… follows.

Conditional Proofs

First Example

Show
If you live in Toronto, then you live in Canada.
Proof
Suppose you live in Toronto. Toronto is a city in Canada, so anyone who lives in Toronto lives in Canada. So, given our assumption, it follows that you live in Canada.

Second Example

Show
If I have hands, then the external world exists.
Proof
Suppose I have hands. Hands are objects in the external world. So, given our assumption, it follows that the external world exists.

Third Example

Show
If the King fails to execute Naṣr al-Dīn, then the King should be executed.
Proof
Suppose the King fails to execute Naṣr al-Dīn. Then Naṣr al-Dīn’s claim, “I will be executed”, was a lie. So the King was lying when he said that all liars will be executed. So, by his own decree, the King should be executed.

Fourth Example

Show
If God exists, then free will is an illusion.
Proof
Suppose God exists. By definition, God is all-knowing, and cannot be mistaken. So, for anything I do, she already knows that I will do it before I do it. But that means I can’t do otherwise: If I could, then God could be mistaken. But she can’t be mistaken. So I can’t. And if I can’t do otherwise, then free will is an illusion.

Indirect Derivations

Basics

  • Indirect Derivation represents an indirect proof, also known as a proof by reductio ad absurdum.
  • The basic idea: to prove that something is false, show that, if it were true, a contradiction would follow.

Indirect Proofs

First Example

Show
It is not the case that God exists.
Proof
Suppose God exists. By definition, she is all-good, all-powerful, and all-knowing. So she won’t allow any evils to occur. But evils do occur. So the assumption that God exists leads to a contradiction. So God doesn’t exist.

First Example

Show
It is not the case that God exists.
Proof
Suppose God exists.
⋮
There is evil.
Not there is evil.

Second Example

Show
Not all sentences are false.
Proof
Assume that all sentences are false. But then the sentence ‘All sentences are false’ is true, which contradicts our assumption.

Second Example

Show
Not all sentences are false.
Proof
Assume that all sentences are false.
⋮
‘All sentences are false’ is true.
Not ‘All sentences are false’ is true.

Third Example

Show
There is no greatest natural number.
Proof
Assume there is a greatest natural number, n. Then n + 1 is also a natural number, and is greater than n. But that contradicts our assumption that n is the greatest natural number.

Third Example

Show
Not there is a greatest natural number.
Proof
Assume there is a greatest natural number, n.
⋮
n + 1 is greater than n
Not n + 1 is greater than n

Theorems

Basics

  • A theorem is a sentence that can be derived from no premises.
  • All theorems are logical truths.
  • Examples: P → P, P → ¬¬P.
