Validity and Formal Validity

Definitions

Validity
An argument is valid iff it is impossible that all of its premises be true, but its conclusion false.
Formal Validity
An argument is formally valid iff every argument that shares its logical form is valid.

Two Arguments

The Pope is an unmarried man.
The Pope is unmarried.
The Pope is a bachelor.
The Pope is unmarried.

Formally Valid

The Pope is an unmarried man.
The Pope is unmarried.
Philosophy is a useless degree.
Philosophy is useless.

Not Formally Valid

The Pope is a bachelor.
The Pope is unmarried.
The Sun is a star.
The Sun is green.

Breakout Activity

  • The point of this activity is to get you thinking about logical form.
  • The Google Doc has several arguments. Some are formally valid, others not.
  • Try to show that they aren’t valid by constructing counterexamples to formal validity: arguments with the same form, that lead from true premises to a false conclusion.

Validity and Ambiguity

Ambiguous Sentences

Ambiguous Sentences
An ambiguous sentence is a sentence that has more than one possible meaning.
  • I caught the fish with the net.
  • It is good or it is bad and I like it.
